I
About this playbook

We ran the Apollo GraphQL migration in April 2026. It went live but it was harder than it had to be: integrations were a black box, custom SF middleware ate 60 unscoped hours, and we kept rediscovering things in week 5 that should have closed in week 1. This document is what we wrote down afterwards.

Fishbowl is the next migration. NewEdge prime, Refindable sub. Engagement runs Mobilize (May 11-21) → Build (May 26-Jun 19) → Migration & Integrations (Jun 22-Jul 10) → UAT (Jun 29-Jul 10) → Target Go-Live July 13. Marketo contract through October as fallback. Numbers below (814 fields, 141 active campaigns of 6,464, 67 forms, 200 templates) are Fishbowl-specific. The methodology is not. Stakeholders, current state, and open decisions live in the Fishbowl decisions log below.

Sources

  • Apollo GraphQL migration (April 2026). Our own field notes.
  • Aptitude8's step-by-step guide. The most thorough public reference. We lean on it for the tactical parts Apollo did not cover (HubSpot UI paths, Marketo actions without direct HubSpot equivalent, suppression-list mechanics, DNS timing).
Apollo lesson

On a tight engagement, the bottleneck is almost never the build. It is client responsiveness and integration audits. Plan around those.

Decisions: closed vs open

II
Pre-kickoff checklist

Get these in writing before signing or starting work. These are derived from the IKT Prep document and from the things Apollo taught us about scope creep.

Get these in writing before signing:

  • Cutover is conditional, not promised. Depends on client responsiveness, Day-1 system access, integration findings during discovery. Date moves if any slips.
  • Day-1 integration audit. Nothing is plug-and-play by default. Every integration classified in week 1 as native, low-effort, medium, or custom-middleware. Hours flow from classification, not the SOW.
  • Hour split between prime and sub. Fishbowl's 150 hours/month across 4 roles is roughly 9 hours/week per person, too thin for execution. Push back before kickoff.
  • GTM audit access. Demand a copy of the upstream audit (Fishbowl: March 2026 Diversis). Every SOW decision sits downstream of it.
  • Contact record scope. Full database or marketable-only? Swings hours by 20-40 percent.
  • List migration scope. SOWs routinely under-scope this. Static, smart, or both.
  • License tier. Aptitude8 is explicit and we agree: Marketing Hub Enterprise + Operations Hub Professional is the floor for this work. Enterprise unlocks calculated properties, sandboxes, and Custom Code Actions. Confirm before discovery starts.

Tooling for week 1: shared Notion/Confluence space, field-mapping spreadsheet skeleton, per-integration intake doc template, Slack channels (joint + sub-only).

Apollo lesson

On Apollo we stepped on the SF admin's toes because nobody had drawn the line between MAP migration and SF schema cleanup. For Fishbowl the risk repeats. Negotiate the boundary in week 1.

III
Discovery Weeks 1 to 2

The first two weeks are not for building. They are for getting an accurate picture of what is actually in Marketo, what is actually in SF, and what the integrations actually do. The SOW numbers (Fishbowl: 814 fields) are a starting point, not the truth.

Marketo audit, five outputs in order

  1. Field inventory. API name, label, type, last-updated date, last-used date, whether it syncs to SF, whether any active campaign or form references it.
  2. Smart campaign inventory. Name, status (active vs paused), last enrolled, trigger type, webhooks yes/no, dependencies.
  3. Form inventory. Name, embed locations, submissions in last 90 days, dedupe behavior, fields collected.
  4. Email template inventory. Name, last edited date, custom-coded sections yes/no, dynamic content yes/no.
  5. LaunchPoint integrations. Every active connection, data direction, trigger, credential validity.

Format: one tab per inventory in a single Google Sheet. Owner: HubSpot Solutions Architect with Marketo admin access.

Pro Tip · Aptitude8

Inside Marketo's Marketing Activities, each smart campaign shows its state through the icon color. Active: Inactive: Use it as your first-pass filter when building the inventory. Anything gray is a "deprecate by default" candidate unless someone explicitly defends it.

Inventory spreadsheet structure

Aptitude8's recommended template, which we adopted, splits the inventory across three tabs:

TabWhat goes in it
Users, Properties & FormsUsers to provision (team + permission set), every Marketo property mapped to its HubSpot counterpart, every form with notification recipients, required fields, hidden fields
Lists & Smart CampaignsLists with Active/Static flag, link to the Marketo list, dependencies (which workflows reference it), folder path; workflows with exact name, link to the Marketo Members List (needed for suppression at go-live)
Emails, Landing Pages & ProgramsEmail templates to rebuild, individual emails with subscription type and template, landing-page templates, landing pages with Marketo and HubSpot links, campaigns/programs

Picklist values: make them match exactly between Marketo and HubSpot. Mismatched casing breaks the sync silently.

Apollo lesson

Fishbowl's SOW says 814 fields. Apollo had roughly 200 of 600+ in real scope. The real number to migrate is almost always around 25 percent of the inventory. Do not let the client convince you everything is critical. Force a Keep/Kill audit on every category.

Salesforce current-state audit

SF stays in place, but the HubSpot to SF sync depends on what is already there. Pull: lifecycle stages (Lead.Status, Opportunity.StageName), the existing Marketo-to-SF field map, custom fields Marketo writes to, validation rules that could block writes, and the integration users with their permission sets.

Integration inventory

Use the HubSpot Integrations General template, one file per integration. For Fishbowl: Clay, ChiliPiper, Warmly, Outreach, Nooks, RingLead, Webflow, LinkedIn LaunchPoint, plus the ad platforms and a Zapier audit. Classify each:

Apollo's SF integration was custom middleware (createSFCampaign.js + syncCampaignMember.js), not native. That added roughly 60 hours that were not in the estimate. For any integration marked TBC, assume custom-middleware effort until proven otherwise.

Discovery answers (live state)

The discovery answers and open items for Fishbowl live in the Fishbowl decisions log at the top of this doc. Closed items there are decisions you can act on. Open items there block phase exit.

Stop and replan if discovery is open at Mobilize end (May 21)

Phase exit requires: field inventory with Keep/Kill signed off, must-migrate campaign list signed off, integrations classified, all "open" items in the Fishbowl decisions log resolved, cutover date confirmed. If any is open at end of Mobilize phase, do not start Build. The cost of replanning now is small. The cost of finding the gap during UAT is the project.

IV
HubSpot foundation

The boring-but-critical phase. Most issues post go-live trace back to skipped steps here.

Portal & tracking

Settings → Account Setup → Account Defaults. Tier (Marketing Hub Enterprise for Fishbowl), time zone, currency, locale, sending domain (DKIM/SPF/DMARC), tracking domain, IP warmup plan if volume justifies it. Configure User Defaults before adding users; defaults applied later do not retroactively update existing accounts.

Tools → Tracking & Analytics → Tracking Code. The four settings Aptitude8 flags and that we have seen go wrong most often:

  • Add every external domain under Advanced Tracking, not just HubSpot-hosted ones
  • Automatic Cross-Domain Tracking on. Without it, traffic from external domains back to HubSpot pages defaults to Direct and the source data is gone
  • Bot Filtering on
  • One Analytics View per domain. Views are not retroactive, so create on day one

Brand Kit & subdomains

Settings → Branding: upload logos in every variant HubSpot requests, define brand colors as named tokens (not hex pasted into 40 places), create one theme and reuse in Design Manager. Decide subdomain conventions in Domains & URLs early: info. / go. / content. for landing pages, email. for email tracking, downloads. for gated assets.

Users, lifecycle, sync

Start from HubSpot's built-in Permission Sets (View Only, Standard User, Content Marketer, Marketing Manager, Sales Manager, Sales Rep, Super Admin) and only build custom sets where needed. Name Teams to match. Lowest permission level that lets us work. MFA enforced.

Lifecycle stages (Fishbowl current state, per Lead Flow Cheatsheet)

The current SF flow that we need to replicate or redefine in HubSpot:

  • New (Lead) → Working (MQL): trigger = enrolled in Outreach sequence OR Chili Piper event added
  • Working → Demo (SAL): trigger = Demo event placed in SF
  • Demo → Converted (SQL): manual, AE converts the Lead in SF
MQL definition is an open decision (Sarah)

Sarah does not want 99.9% of leads becoming MQL just because they entered an Outreach sequence (Discovery 4). The HubSpot MQL trigger will likely be more restrictive than the current SF rule, probably tied to the scoring tier (Section IX). Do not wire lifecycle automations until Sarah signs off on the new MQL definition.

Sync configuration

HubSpot to Salesforce sync: install the official integration, authenticate with a dedicated integration user (never a real person), default Fishbowl inclusion list is marketable contacts only, conflict resolution defined per field, sync errors dashboard pinned with an owner. Activate in test mode on a handful of records, validate, then turn on full sync. Source of truth per field defined in the mapping doc before turning sync on (see Apollo lesson below).

Email opt-out / unsubscribe (compliance)

Today email_opt_out syncs bidirectional SF ↔ Marketo ↔ Outreach. Post-cutover, HubSpot becomes source of truth and the sync to Outreach must be preserved. If this breaks, SDRs receive opt-out complaints and we have a potential GDPR/CAN-SPAM exposure. Validate the subscription preference round-trip (HubSpot unsubscribe → SF contact opt-out → Outreach exclude) in sandbox before go-live.

Apollo lesson

Apollo had lifecycle drift because both sides could write without conflict rules. Define source of truth per field in the mapping doc before turning sync on.

V
Field migration (clean rebuild)

The principle: do not migrate fields. Migrate the records, and let the active fields populate naturally. Bring along only what HubSpot needs to function or what reporting continuity requires.

Object mapping (Fishbowl-specific)

The generic Aptitude8 mapping is misleading for Fishbowl because Salesforce uses Lead AND Contact as separate objects, and HubSpot has neither distinction. The real mapping:

HContact (pre-SQL)
⇄ SSF Lead
HContact (post-SQL / converted)
⇄ SSF Contact
HCompany
⇄ SSF Account (default sync; no custom parent/child hierarchy in scope)
HDeal
Out of scope. Sales is self-sourcing in SF, Opportunities do not sync to HubSpot. If marketing needs commercial visibility, expose as synced Contact properties (sf_last_opp_stage, sf_last_opp_amount) one-way SF→HS.
The Lead-to-Contact pivot at conversion

This is the most fragile part of the native HS↔SF sync. When an AE converts an SF Lead to an SF Contact, the same human now exists under a different SF object. HubSpot still has one Contact record. The integration needs to know to start syncing to the new SF Contact ID instead of the archived Lead ID.

To survive the pivot:

  • Store the HubSpot Contact ID on both Lead.HubSpot_Contact_ID__c and Contact.HubSpot_Contact_ID__c so the integration can find its way back from either side.
  • Configure the SF connector's inclusion criteria to include Leads and Contacts, not one or the other.
  • Validate end-to-end with a test conversion in sandbox before go-live. The most common failure mode is a duplicate HubSpot Contact created post-conversion because the integration lost the link.

Do not recreate what HubSpot ships with

HubSpot defaults align with Marketo defaults for almost all common fields. Recreating them as custom properties is the most common rookie mistake. The ones that map directly:

First & Last Name · Email Address · Phone Number · City, State, Zip · Job Title · Company Name

If Marketo has a custom property by the same name as a HubSpot default, map to the default. Do not create a parallel custom property.

Deprecation pass

For everything that is not a default-to-default mapping, kill dead fields in Marketo first: orphaned fields (Fishbowl: 20), duplicate clusters (Fishbowl: 59), fields not referenced by any active campaign/form/workflow, fields with no value in any record in the last 12 months. Output: a Migrate / Keep in Marketo / Deprecate decision per field, signed off by marketing and the SF admin.

Field mapping spreadsheet

One row per surviving field. Columns: marketo_name, marketo_label, marketo_type, hubspot_name, hubspot_label, hubspot_type, hubspot_group, sf_name, direction, source_of_truth, transformation, migration_required (Y/N), owner.

Naming: snake_case for internal names. Apply the prefix policy from Section IV. Picklist values must match casing across HubSpot and SF; mismatches fail silently.

Property creation order

Create every target HubSpot property before running any list or contact import. The import flow maps CSV columns to existing properties; if the target is missing, the only path is to abort, create it, and restart. Aptitude8 flags this as one of the most common time-loss mistakes in data migration.

SF validation rules to capture as required fields

Whitney called out that SF blocks Lead → Demo conversion if certain fields are missing or invalid (e.g., accounting_system empty, company_name = "no", country in a disqualified list). These rules become silent sync failures if HubSpot writes a record that does not pass SF validation. Capture them in the field mapping spreadsheet under a sf_required_for_conversion column and reflect them as required fields on the relevant HubSpot forms.

Country-based disqualification, which Marketo handles automatically today, migrates as either (a) a HubSpot workflow that sets a disqualified property when country is in the unsupported list, or (b) an SF validation rule. Decide which side owns it before cutover.

Attribution properties

SF is first-touch today; marketing wants visibility of both. Define two HubSpot properties in the mapping doc:

  • original_source: first-touch, set on contact create, never overwritten. Maps to SF Lead's first-touch field.
  • recent_source: last-touch, updated on every new engagement. Sync direction HS→SF.
VI
Forms migration

Fishbowl has 67 approved Marketo forms. Apollo had a different scale but the same problem: forms accumulate, dedupe is inconsistent, embeds are scattered.

Audit, kill, merge

For each form: under 5 submissions in 90 days → propose deprecation; no live embed → deprecate; duplicates doing the same job → merge; field list bloated → cut to the routing minimum. Target: 67 forms down to 30 to 40 production forms before rebuilding.

Dedupe

Fishbowl's audit flagged an 8 percent contact duplicate rate from forms without dedupe. Configure HubSpot's email-based dedupe (default on submit), call RingLead's API on submit if it stays as the dedupe authority, decide hard-match behavior per form (update existing is the usual choice, except for contact-us forms which always create).

Embed strategy during transition

Fishbowl's website is also migrating (Unbounce to Webflow in July). Forms may need to live in two places during the window. Identify which forms stay on the current site until cutover, plan the dual-embed (HubSpot code on Webflow at switchover, parallel-run period where both write to HubSpot), confirm the HubSpot tracking code is on every page that hosts a HubSpot form or attribution breaks.

VII
Campaigns & Salesforce sync The big one

This is the most expensive workstream in any Marketo migration. Fishbowl has 141 active campaigns out of 6,464 total. Apollo's numbers were smaller, the pattern is identical.

Kill before rebuild

Before any HubSpot build, run a Kill/Keep on the 141 active campaigns. Deprecate anything that enrolled fewer than 10 contacts in 90 days, triggers a deprecated workflow, or was a one-off for a past event. Merge duplicates with minor variations. Migrate the rest. Target: 141 down to 60-70 production workflows, signed off by the marketing lead.

Apollo lesson

Apollo's client had the same "we can't turn anything off" instinct. When we forced the audit, 60 percent of active campaigns had not enrolled a contact in 90 days. Push hard on this in week 3.

Marketo flow actions with no direct HubSpot equivalent

These are the patterns Aptitude8 documents and we have hit in practice. Memorize the workarounds; rebuilding workflows is faster when you stop searching for an equivalent that does not exist.

MMarketo actionHHubSpot approach
MChange Program Status HNo direct equivalent. Assign the workflow to the corresponding HubSpot Campaign. Campaign association is how you preserve "this contact participated in X."
MChange Data Value (conditional) HMarketo's "if X then set Y" inline action does not exist as one step. Use an If/Then branch followed by a Set Property Value action, then a Go-To action to rejoin the main flow.
MRequest Campaign HMaps cleanly to Enroll in another workflow.
MCampaign is Requested (trigger) HUse HubSpot's Enrollment Triggers. Setup Triggers → Workflows → Enrolled in Workflow → Enrolled By Workflow → select the source workflow. For legacy trigger systems, enable manual enrollment and turn on re-enrollment.
MRemove From Flow HUse the Goals feature on the workflow. The Goal acts as an automatic un-enroll: any contact that meets the Goal criteria is removed. Infer the goal criteria from the same conditions that triggered the Marketo Remove From Flow.

HubSpot to Salesforce sync (Fishbowl approach)

HubSpot is the system of record for campaign attribution. We use only the native HubSpot to Salesforce integration that was configured in Section IV. No custom code, no Workflow Custom Code Actions, no per-campaign middleware.

Practically that means:

  • Contacts, leads, accounts and opportunities sync via the native integration
  • Campaign membership lives on the HubSpot side; HubSpot Campaigns are the source of truth
  • If sales needs SFDC-side visibility into campaign engagement, expose it as a synced contact property (last_campaign_engaged, campaign_count_30d) rather than mirroring HubSpot Campaigns to Salesforce Campaign objects

Historical Marketo data

Pre-cutover form fills do not move into HubSpot via the migration. After cutover, HubSpot's Forms tab shows only post-cutover submissions. If the client needs historical engagement inside HubSpot, the only path is a one-time CSV import of Marketo activity logs, which is a separate workstream and a change order by default. Set this expectation in week 1.

VIII
Email templates

200 approved templates in Marketo, almost all from 2021 to 2022. The clean-rebuild approach applies even harder here than for fields or campaigns.

Keep/Kill

Kill anything not used in the last 12 months, anything with custom HTML/CSS that does not render cleanly in HubSpot's drag-and-drop, and any duplicates with minor variations. Target production set: under 30 templates.

Rebuild approach

Start from a HubSpot pre-built drag-and-drop template that matches the Marketo original (Aptitude8 makes this explicit, and we agree their stock templates cover almost every Marketo layout we have rebuilt). Replicate sender, personalization tokens, and dynamic content blocks. Save reusable pieces (header, footer, CTA) as saved sections. Test through Litmus or HubSpot's preview-by-recipient before approving.

Subscription types & sender strategy

Define subscription types in Tools → Marketing → Email before any email work begins. Aptitude8's useful point: you do not have to mirror Marketo's. The migration is the cheapest moment to consolidate. Collapse to the smallest set that lets users choose per-channel intent (Newsletter, Product Updates, Events) plus one or two regional variants if consent law requires.

Sender strategy is its own decision (Fishbowl SOW flagged "30 sender personas" as a concern). Consolidate to one brand sender, regional senders, or per-CSM senders, configure HubSpot sending domains accordingly, document which template uses which sender.

IX
Lead scoring Fishbowl-specific

Apollo did not implement scoring during its migration. The Fishbowl audit produced a validated 3-axis model that needs deployment.

Model summary (from the audit)

Inbound
Lead Source (67 pts) + Accounting System (31 pts) + Industry (2 pts)
Outbound
Accounting System (94 pts) + Industry (6 pts)

Tier thresholds

TierScoreRoutingExpected win rate
Tier 1100+ ptsAuto-route to AE, skip SDR33.8 percent
Tier 260 to 99 ptsStandard SDR qualification23 to 28 percent
Tier 330 to 59 pts60-day nurture, SDR if capacity18.2 percent
Tier 4<30 ptsAutomated nurture only1 to 5 percent

Gates before deployment (strict order)

Do not deploy scoring on dirty data. Execute the gates in order; each one is a precondition for the next. Risk R6 in Section XIV is what happens if you skip this sequence.

  1. Resolve the 14 Clay → SFDC writeback race conditions (one Clay table per SF field).
  2. Activate Clay writeback in Phase 1 (777 accounts).
  3. Normalize Lead Source across Marketo, HubSpot, and SF.
  4. Configure the scoring property in HubSpot (formula, calculated tier property).
  5. Backfill the score on all in-scope contacts and validate the distribution against the audit's predictions.
  6. Activate tier-based routing in ChiliPiper (or SF lead-assignment).

Scoring does not turn on until gates 1-5 are green. This is a visible check, not a soft preference. Routing in step 6 is the last switch flipped.

Deployment

Build the formula in HubSpot's Score property (or HubSpot Score on Enterprise). Inbound and Outbound become two separate properties if the model differs. Tier is a calculated property based on the score, synced to SF for routing.

Scope note: the model below applies to NA and UK. The SOW flags that APAC requires separate development. APAC scoring is deferred to hypercare or a change order; do not attempt to fit one model across regions.

Two HubSpot caveats Aptitude8 calls out

1. There is no "set score to X" or "reset to zero" in HubSpot. Marketo lets you set the score to a literal value (e.g., "Lead Score = 50 on form submit"). HubSpot only supports increments and decrements. Workaround: when the equivalent Marketo rule sets the score to N, configure HubSpot to add N on the same trigger. The math comes out the same as long as you start from a known baseline.

2. Use date ranges to prevent runaway scores. Instead of "+10 every time a contact fills out a form" (which grows forever), configure "+10 only if the form submission happened in the last 30 days." The 30-day window means old engagement decays naturally without writing a separate decay workflow.

X
Integrations remapping

The single biggest source of timeline risk after campaigns. Fishbowl has 21 integrations, 8 marked TBC.

Do not use the Marketo to HubSpot Sync App for migration

Aptitude8 is unambiguous on this and matches what we saw. The official sync app only handles text-field properties on the Contact object. No companies, no deals, no picklists, no booleans, no dates, no custom objects. It is fine as a short-term coexistence bridge while both systems run, but it is not a migration tool. Move historical data via Marketo CSV exports or programmatic API transfer.

Day-1 classification & quick wins

Every integration classified by end of week 1 using the table from Section III. If we cannot classify it (no access, no docs), that is itself a finding and escalates. Quick win for Fishbowl: reauthorize LinkedIn LaunchPoint (Lead Gen Forms expired 7/31/2025, Matched Audiences expired 1/23/2026) in Marketo as a week-1 task, then plan the HubSpot remap.

Out of scope (state it explicitly so nobody scopes it back in)

  • Renewal pipeline visibility in HubSpot. Marketing does not need it. Defaults apply.
  • Custom parent-child account hierarchy mapping. SF has it, HubSpot's parent/child Company model is not 1:1, default sync behavior is what we use.
  • Opportunity / Deal sync. Covered in Section V. Sales is self-sourcing in SF.

Compliance: Outreach unsubscribe sync

Cross-referenced from Section IV. Today email_opt_out is bidirectional SF ↔ Marketo ↔ Outreach. Post-cutover the chain becomes HubSpot (source of truth) → SF → Outreach. Test the round-trip in sandbox before go-live: unsubscribe in HubSpot must reflect in Outreach's exclude list within the documented sync window. If this breaks at production scale, SDRs will surface it within hours and we have a CAN-SPAM / GDPR exposure.

Monitoring

For every integration that goes live: failed-sync dashboard with an owner, alert rule for credential expiration, runbook entry for failure recovery, kill switch documented.

XI
Lists & segmentation

The SOW barely mentions this. Treat it as its own workstream or it will eat a week of unscoped time.

Audit & rebuild

Inventory all Marketo Smart Lists and Static Lists. Keep what is referenced by surviving campaigns (Section VII), deprecate the rest. Smart Lists become HubSpot Active Lists, Static Lists become HubSpot Static Lists. Re-validate membership after rebuild because the criteria can produce different results in HubSpot.

For Active Lists, check the Marketo source's Smart List tab for whether it uses "Use ALL Filters" or advanced rules: that tells you whether the HubSpot rebuild is a flat AND or needs explicit OR branches. Reuse the exact Marketo name on the HubSpot list so the QA pair is searchable.

Exporting from Marketo

Static lists move via export and re-import (the sync app does not cover them). Per Aptitude8: in Marketo, open the list and click List Actions → Export List; for a full DB dump use Database → System Smart Lists → All People. Pick columns deliberately because exports with every column on a large DB take many hours. Import to HubSpot only after the target properties exist (Section V).

Subscriptions per region

Fishbowl operates across NA, APAC, UK with potentially different consent flows. Map Marketo subscription types to HubSpot, configure unsubscribe pages per region if required, migrate consent state (subscribed vs unsubscribed) per region.

XII
Cutover plan

The transition from "Marketo is production" to "HubSpot is production." Fishbowl's accelerated timeline targets late June or early July. The Marketo contract runs through October, which gives a fallback buffer.

Sandbox testing before any live cutover

If the client is on HubSpot Enterprise, sync all the rebuilt assets into a HubSpot Sandbox first and dry-run the workflows on test contacts. The Sandbox is the only safe place to QA without risking marketing sends to real contacts.

Sandbox caveat (Aptitude8)

HubSpot Sandboxes do not sync changes back up to Production. Any fix or tweak made in the Sandbox during QA has to be re-applied to Production by hand. Keep a running change log during the Sandbox QA and have someone responsible for replaying it in Production before go-live.

Suppression Lists Mission critical

Aptitude8 calls this step "mission critical" and we have seen the bill when it gets skipped: contacts who already completed a Marketo nurture get pulled into the same nurture in HubSpot at go-live, and the same person receives the same email twice. To prevent it:

  1. For every workflow you are migrating, export the corresponding Marketo Members List (you already linked these in the inventory spreadsheet during discovery).
  2. Upload each Members List into HubSpot as a Static List. Name the list to match the workflow it suppresses.
  3. Open the matching HubSpot workflow, go to Settings → Un-enrollment and Suppression, and add the static list under "Suppression Lists for this workflow."
  4. Skip this step only for workflows that explicitly allow re-enrollment (e.g., form-submission triggers where multiple submissions per contact are by design).

Parallel-run & letting Marketo finish

Recommended window: mid-July to mid-August. HubSpot is system of record from cutover forward. Marketo stays active but launches nothing new. Website forms submit to BOTH systems during the parallel window. Lead routing comes from HubSpot only. End of parallel-run: Marketo forms removed from the site, Marketo campaigns archived but readable.

For Marketo campaigns with contacts in wait steps at cutover: do not deactivate (Aptitude8's recommendation, matches Apollo's experience). Remove the Smart List criteria so no new contacts enroll, but leave the campaign live until existing members complete. Deactivating mid-flow strands contacts between systems and the cleanup is worse than the wait.

DNS & URL redirects (T-day mechanics)

These three steps must happen on cutover day, in this order, and the gap between them measured in minutes, not hours. If Marketo pages stay reachable while DNS points to HubSpot, you get half-broken forms in production.

  1. Update DNS records for the subdomains you chose in Section IV (Foundation). Point them at HubSpot.
  2. Add a Redirected Domain in HubSpot's Domains & URLs settings if the new HubSpot subdomain differs from the old Marketo one. This tells HubSpot to accept traffic on the old hostname.
  3. Add URL redirects one by one in the Redirects tab. Original URL is the full old Marketo URL; Redirect To is the full new HubSpot URL. Use Standard Redirects for most pages.
HubSpot must host both domains

Aptitude8 spells this out and it bites every team that learns it the hard way: HubSpot can only redirect a URL it hosts. If the old Marketo subdomain has not been added to Domains & URLs (and DNS does not point at HubSpot for it), the redirect rule will not fire. After adding each redirect, give it up to 5 minutes to propagate and verify in a private/incognito window to bypass local cache.

Kill switches, rollback, comms

Every HubSpot workflow has a documented kill switch. Lead routing rules have a fallback owner. Rollback: define what "going back to Marketo" means in the first 48 hours before cutover (realistically rollback is per-system, never full). Comms: pre-cutover briefings for marketing, sales, and IT/SF admin; cutover war-room channel active for 72 hours post-launch.

XIII
Hypercare

30 hours/month for 6 months post-cutover.

Covers: triage of sync errors, workflow misfires, data discrepancies, small adjustments to workflows and mappings, reporting tweaks, training reinforcement.

Does not cover: net-new features, new integrations, bulk data corrections beyond drift. Anything outside scope triggers a change order.

Ops: weekly standup with client RevOps lead, shared issue tracker, monthly KPI review (sync error rate, MQL volume, attribution coverage). Month 6: formal handoff with documentation refresh and future roadmap conversation.
